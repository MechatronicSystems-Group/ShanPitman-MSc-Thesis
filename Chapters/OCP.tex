\chapter{Optimal Camera Placement}
\label{OCP}
The quality of a multi-camera localisation system is determined as much by the geometric arrangement as by hardware. Sensor noise, calibration accuracy, and algorithmic performance all matter; yet even a perfectly calibrated, high-quality camera system running a sophisticated algorithm can still fail to localise the target if its cameras are arranged poorly. Determining the \gls{OCP} requires both a model of how error arises and subsequently propagates, and an effective optimsation formulation that is capable of navigating a large search space timeously. 



When the sensor placement is suboptimal, the inaccuracy tends to compound in two ways. The first results from the geometric measurement uncertainty. Cameras discretise their worldview into pixels; the target is projected onto a finite grid of pixels, and its position is inferred from this quantised image (as explained in \ref{sec:CameraModelProjection}). The further a target is from a camera, the fewer pixels it subtends, reducing the spatial resolution and increasing the resulting position estimate's variance. Moreover, when two cameras observe a target from nearly parallel viewing angles, small pixel-level uncertainties preclude triangulation \parencite{sharmaAnalysisUncertaintyBounds1998}. The resulting depth estimate becomes ill-conditioned, degrading the tracking prediction. Hence, in these avoidable configurations, the limitation of the tracking system is not the sensor itself but the geometry.



The second degrading source is visibility loss. Intuitively a camera cannot measure what it cannot see. Occlusion, whether caused by invariant static obstacles in the environment or dynamically occurring by the target itself reduce the number of measurements \parencite{rahimianOptimalCameraPlacement2017}. Similarly, limited coverage may result in area's being unobserved. Therefore, it may be necessary to optimise for an arrangement that rewards redundant views whilst still ensuring that most of the flight space is observable \parencite{aissaouiDesigningCameraPlacement2018}. Thus, the arrangement of the sensors can exacerbate localisation error beyond the intrinsic noise characteristics of the cameras themselves. 



Optimising placement, however, is combinatorially explosive, and classified as \gls{NP-hard} \parencite{kirchhofOptimalPlacementMultiple2013}. As the number of inputs (camera, candidate placements, and workspace size) grow, the number of possible configurations grows exponentially; consequently, an exhaustive search becomes intractable.  In this context, \gls{NP-hard} denotes that the problem becomes increasingly computationally challenging to find a guaranteed global solution with a multitude of inputs. The literature suggests that heuristic- or metaheauristic-based algorithms, such as genetic algorithms, greedy methods, or \gls{PSO} approaches, are adopted because of their proficiency in exploring large, unsmooth, multi-objective search spaces efficiently [Citation needed]. Alternatives, like integer and mixed-integer programming attempt to find globally optimal solutions but under more heavily imposed constraints. Therefore, each optimisation technique reflects a tradeoff between optimality guarantees, scalability, efficiency and modelling fidelity.



\section{Literature Review}   
Optimising camera placement is not optional; it should be a prerequisite for accurate tracking. This section reviews the literature on the \gls{OCP} problem. First, the optimisation strategies employed to search the ill-defined, non-convex search space in sufficiently good time are examined. Second, an examination of how the sources of measurement uncertainty, quantisation, triangulation geometry,  and occlusion, have been characterized and modelled is provided.  Together, this provides the state of the art in designing geometrically valid and computationally reasonable multi-camera tracking systems.



\subsection{Background of OCP}
\gls{OCP} descends from the well-studied \gls{AGP}, which established the combinatorial foundations of visibility-based sensor placement. The \gls{AGP} minimises the number of omnidirectional vertex guards required to observe an entire 2D polygon with $n$ vertices. However, the \gls{AGP}'s usefulness is bounded by its assumptions: where guard are endowed with unlimited sensing range for 360 $\degree$ \gls{FOV}, and perfect visibility. Real cameras violate these assumptions; where physical sensors have a limited range of marker detection, resolution that degrades with distance, a finite \gls{FOV}, and the target may be occluded during observation. 



The \gls{AGP} is bounded by its approach to solely guarantee that all the points in the workspace are seen by idealised observers. Whereas, the \gls{OCP} objective is to determine the most accurate camera configuration to accurately reconstruct the points. This shift highlights how the \gls{OCP} frames the problem as one to ensure quality of reconstruction rather than feasibility. In \gls{MoCap}, a marker that is only visible by one camera cannot be triangulated; Olague and Mohr emphasise how a marker visible by two (anti)parallel cameras can be triangulated but with large uncertainty in the depth direction; and a marker seen by two or more cameras with sufficient angular separation yields a well-conditioned reconstruction \parencite{olagueOptimalCameraPlacement2002}. Therefore, although the \gls{OCP} inherits the NP-hard structure of the \gls{AGP} it is necessary for the optimisation to encode that every point of interest is visible by a sufficient number of cameras in a geometrically favourable arrangement rather than described as a binary coverage test.  



\subsection{Motivation for Optimal Placement}
The need for \gls{OCP} emerges across diverse applications, each imposing different constraints on what embodies optimal placement. Surveillance and monitoring systems are most closely connected to the \gls{AGP} formulation, where spatial coverage is maximised to ensure that no region goes unobserved, tolerating lower reconstruction accuracy in exchange for exhaustiveness \parencite{bodorOptimalCameraPlacement2007}. By contrast, \gls{MoCap} systems require each marker in the scene to be visible to at least two cameras, with sufficent angular separation simultaneously for accurate triangulation  \parencite{aissaouiDesigningCameraPlacement2018, zieglerOptimalCameraPlacement2023, zhouSensorPlacementOptimization2024}. Autonomous driving scenarios necessitate a full $360 \degree$ surround view, stitching adjacent camera frustrums to provide the driver  and autonomous system with reliable proximity warnings of neighbouring obstacles \parencite{puligandlaMultiresolutionApproachLarge2022}. Augmented and virtual reality applications face more severe mounting constraints and an abundance of unpredictable dynamic occlusion, as the human user moves freely within the encapsulating simulation equipment \parencite{rahimianOptimalCameraPlacement2017}. Despite this diversity, all camera placement optimisers in the literature share the common challenge: the quality of the system's output (surveillance or surrounding map, reconstructed trajectory, or a pose estimate) hinges on where the cameras are placed.


Without a formal optimiser, camera networks are typicaaly set-up through ad-hoc designer intuition or rule-of-thumb  guidelines, for e.g. distribute the cameras evenly around the perimeter. While this may appear as an efficient approach, the designer has no means of assessing whether a different arrangement would reduce localisation error. The resulting configuration is neither verifiably optimal nor is the theoretical performance quantified. This is particularly pertinent when the number of cameras is constrained by budget, as each camera should be placed to contribute advantageously to the overall network. Rahimian et al. demonstrated that the performance cost of forgoing optimisation can be substantial. Their \gls{PSO} \gls{OCP} algorithm enabled their OptiTrack system to reconstruct all 37 markers on 86\% of captured frames, compared to only 30\% under an ad-hoc configuration \parencite{rahimianOptimalCameraPlacement2017}. This nearly tripled improvement achieved by repositioning the cameras in the same room illustrates the performance gap between optimised and unguided placements. 


Thus, the motivation to optimise is evident but the problem itself is involved. The \gls{AGP}, from which the \gls{OCP} descends, is NP-hard even under its idealised two-dimensional, panoptic-guard assumptions \parencite{kirchhofOptimalPlacementMultiple2013}. Subsequently, the \gls{OCP} is even more complex since it requires more inputs: each camera is described both by a three-dimensional position $(x,y,z)$ and orientation $(\theta, \phi, \psi)$, and the sensor has constraints, such as a finite field of view and depth of field that the \gls{AGP} does not consider [Citation NEEDED/ section reference here about AGP being NP hard and thus with OCP having more constraints it becomes increasingly challenging]. The placement of $k$ cameras over a subsampled three-dimensional workspace creates a search space that grows combinatorially. If the mounting locations are discretised, further reducing the cardinality of the problem, into $N$ candidate positions, each allowing $M$ candidate orientations, then the number of distinct camera configurations is $\binom{NM}{k}$. For a modest $N=50$ possible positions, $M= 8$ orientations, and $k=6$ cameras this exceeds $5^{12}$ combinations; every one of which requires its own assessment based on the cost function which involves visibility quality and or triangulation geometry computations. Consequently, most investigators resort to heuristic-based approximation algorithms which, albeit without global optimality guarantees, offer the only practical path to obtain acceptable solutions within reasonable computation time.

\subsubsection{Optimisation Objectives}
Depending on the application, available resources, and workspace constraints, different optimisation objectives have been prioritised in the literature. Broadly, these can be categorised into coverage-based objectives and accuracy-based objectives. In terms of coverage-based objectives, the goal is to maximise the visible area of the workspace, and sometimes going further to handle aspects of occlusion both static and dynamic and hence usually ensuring a two-cover coverage. This is particularly relevant in surveillance applications where ensuring that all regions are monitored is critical \parencite{bodorOptimalCameraPlacement2007}. Conversely, accuracy-based objectives focus on minimising localisation error, which is crucial in motion capture and augmented reality applications where precise tracking is required and more than one camera arranged sufficiently non-parallel is needed to ensure triangulation is possible \parencite{aissaouiDesigningCameraPlacement2018, rahimianOptimalCameraPlacement2017}. Wu, Sharma, and Huang proposed another accuracy-based metric of placement which assessed how the limited camera intrinsics and quantisation lead to inaccurate measurements exacerbated by the placement of two cameras \parencite{sharmaAnalysisUncertaintyBounds1998}. Many studies have proposed hybrid objectives that balance the trade-off of accuracy with coverage \parencite{zieglerOptimalCameraPlacement2023, zhouSensorPlacementOptimization2024}. Some works have also considered additional constraints such as budget limits and physical mounting options \parencite{aissaouiDesigningCameraPlacement2018, puligandlaMultiresolutionApproachLarge2022, daiOptimalCameraConfiguration}.
The configuration quality for 3D marker tracking and trinagulation is mostly affected by two main sources of error: marker visibility and triangulation accuracy. In terms of the application being \gls{MoCap}, a marker should not be occluded by any obstacle, including itself, the marker must be within the cameras field of view, vissible given the camera's resolution and range of capture. These are necessary to ensure consistent and continual tracking. Furthermore, at least two cameras should be arranged sufficiently non-parallel so that triangulation is well-conditioned. Sometimes, the easy answer is just to throw more cameras at the problem as that does give redundant views and ensures we can place camera pairs to cover the scene in a sufficently non-parallel format but in most research situationships, it is not prudent to over-exhaust the budget and hence it may be a desirable goal to asses the least amount of cameras required to achieve a level of accuracy/ coverage to minimise cost.

The optimal camera placement problem extends the \gls{AGP} foundation by introducing constraints that reflect the true capabilities and limitations of physical sensors. These extensions transform camera placement from a geometric covering problem into a constrained optimisation problem where the cost function must encode both visibility and measurement quality

Generally, the OCP can be stated as follows: given a set of candidate camera locations and orientations, find the configuration that maximises 3D reconstruction quality of a target within a defined workspace, subject to practical constraints such as mounting options and budget. Solving this problem requires balancing trade-offs among coverage, accuracy, and computational efficiency. Without the aid of an optimisation algorithm, the sensors are arranged in an ad-hoc configuration by the designer, which requires expertise or trial and error, which can be time-consuming or sacrificial if a sub-par arrangement is settled on with no mathematical  quantitative guidance. Thus, the resulting localisation error is neither calculated nor considered mathematically. Here lies the need to optimise the camera network based on metrics that minimise degrading effects like resolution uncertainty and probabilistic occlusion even if the trajectory of the target is not known exactly beforehand.
